Open Source Guiding Principles

Michelin has been committed to open source for many years, and our journey continues to evolve.

There are three main open source activities at Michelin:

  • Consume: using third-party open source software in Michelinâ€™s technology stack. We're more than ever committed to use open source software and we already have an open source review board led by DOTI/SMI & DCD teams. It mainly ensures that when using an open source software, we check the license, terms and conditions and specific legal issues (such as embargos â€¦).

  • Contribute: contributing to existing third-party open source projects. These contributions happened several times in the past and we do have a legal framework we can use. It covers the license under which we contribute, which legal company is contributing, the individual that contributes and the intellectual property management. We would like to encourage our teams to contribute on open source projects: it's a way for Michelin to give back to the OSS community after years consuming the value it produced.

  • Create: open sourcing proprietary software and their full lifecycle, both internally and externally. Michelin is now willing to create open source software and it's a strong inflexion in this area.

Michelin is transforming into a software-centric company and, in this shift, open-source software (OSS) plays a crucial role in achieving software excellence. Therefore, we are embracing an Open Source first strategy to foster Michelinâ€™s profitable and sustainable growth.

The four main drivers behind our Open Source adoption and promotion are:

  • Engineering Economics: We recognize that it is more cost-effective to thoughtfully use or customize existing OSS than to build our own solutions to compete with an already successful Open Source community. Furthermore, this economics equation also exists for commercial off-the-shelf (COTS): In some cases, adopting OSS is competitive compared to commercial offers. Over the long term, leveraging existing software accelerates development, allows shared development, and optimizes R&D. OSS offers also significant advantages in reducing vendor lock-in. They typically adhere to open standards, ensuring compatibility across different platforms and technologies. This reduces the risk of getting locked into a specific vendor's ecosystem. Unlike proprietary software with a single editor, there are often multiple vendors offering support and services for popular open-source projects. This fosters competition, giving you the option to switch providers without being tied to one.

  • Talent Acquisition and Retention: By showcasing how Michelin creates innovative technologies that solve technical problems, we can further learn and grow our technical expertise, cultivate Michelinâ€™s reputation and gain influence within the community. In turn, this leads to greater talent acquisition and retention. Likewise, recruiting benefits from a pool of external talents with existing knowledge of the Open Source software already used in our technology stack.

  • Innovation and flexibility Open source fuels innovation through collaboration, faster development cycles, transparency, and lower costs. It fosters a community of developers that continuously improve and iterate on open-source projects.

  • Innovation and flexibility we benefited from by contributing and giving back.
